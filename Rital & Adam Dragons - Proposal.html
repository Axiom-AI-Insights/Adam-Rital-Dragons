The Opportunity

The UK property investment market is going through a structural shift. The old ways of doing things are breaking down and a gap is opening up that nobody is filling properly.

Landlords are leaving - fast

The numbers tell a clear story. Regulatory pressure, rising costs and tax changes are pushing landlords - especially smaller, traditional ones - towards the exit.

What's driving this?

⚠
Regulation
Section 21 abolition changes everything

From 1 May 2026, landlords can no longer serve no-fault evictions. This removes a key tool for managing problem tenancies AND for gaining vacant possession to carry out EPC improvements. A landlord who needs their property empty to retrofit it to Band C now has a much harder path to get there. For many smaller landlords, this is the tipping point.

£
Costs
EPC upgrades cost serious money

Retrofitting an older property to EPC Band C typically costs between £10,000 and £20,000+. For a landlord with two or three properties and mortgage rates that have doubled since 2021, that's a bill they may simply not be able to pay. The deadline is 2030, but the financial pressure is already here.

📈
Tax
Tax increases from April 2027

Property income tax rates are set to rise by 2 percentage points above other income from April 2027. Combined with Making Tax Digital requirements (quarterly digital filing from April 2026 for landlords earning £50k+), the administrative and financial burden is increasing. 2.4 million landlords are affected.

The niche nobody is serving

There's a specific group of landlords who are being left behind.

Distressed, traditional and old money landlords who bought their properties years ago and have been managing them the same way ever since. They don't know about modern strategies like Rent to Rent, Purchase Lease Options, serviced accommodation or HMO conversions. They hear about EPC deadlines and Section 21 changes and their instinct is to sell up because they don't know there are alternatives.

This group has assets and money. What they lack is knowledge and options. That's exactly where this business sits.

The education gap

On the other side, there are thousands of aspiring property investors who've been through training courses - Wealth Dragons, Property Investor Network, Touchstone Education, Property CEO and others. These courses teach the theory: how to find deals, how to structure investments, how property strategies work.

But then the training ends and graduates are left to figure out the actual doing on their own. No deal pipeline, no analysis tools, no support network, no hand-holding through their first purchase. The education-to-execution gap is where most people get stuck, lose confidence or never take the first step.

The opportunity in a sentence: traditional landlords who have properties but lack knowledge, and trained investors who have knowledge but lack properties. Nobody is joining them up properly.

Platforms exist, but they're incomplete

There are established players doing parts of this well. SiteFindr (since 2012) runs a marketplace connecting deal sourcers with investors - deals sell within 48-72 hours and they charge £5k+VAT per deal. PropertyData, PropertyEngine and Realyse provide data and analysis tools. PropMarker uses AI to scan listings automatically.

But these are all point solutions. A sourcer uses one tool to find deals, another to analyse them, then sources their own finance, negotiates independently and manages the property themselves. Nobody provides the full picture - the single front door where you bring a property situation and get guided to the right outcome with the right people.

The Concept

A property solutions network that helps people do something smart with what they have - and connects them to the right people to make it happen.

"Bring us your property issue."

That's the proposition. Whether you're a landlord drowning in regulation, an investor looking for your first deal, or a family dealing with an inherited property - you come to us, we diagnose the situation, show you the options and connect you with trusted people who can execute.

How It Works

Five steps from first contact to ongoing relationship. Every client gets a structured process, not a sales pitch.

1

Diagnose

Understand the client's situation. What do they have? What do they want? What are their constraints? This isn't a quick form - it's a proper assessment. A landlord with three terraced houses and no EPC compliance is a completely different situation to a first-time investor with £50k cash looking for a BMV deal. The diagnosis determines everything that follows.

AI role: Automated portfolio analysis, regulatory compliance checking, market valuation, yield calculations - all done before the first conversation so we arrive with data, not just questions.

2

Advise

Present the options clearly. Not one recommendation - a range of paths with honest trade-offs. "You could sell, and here's what you'd likely get. You could convert to serviced accommodation, and here's the yield. You could lease to a social housing provider, and here's the guaranteed income. You could Rent to Rent. Here are the costs, timelines and risks of each."

AI role: Generate scenario comparisons, financial projections and regulatory impact assessments for each option. What used to take a consultant a week takes minutes.

3

Connect

Once the client chooses a path, connect them to the right people in the network. Selling? Here's a vetted investor who buys in that area. Converting to SA? Here's Rital's management company. Need finance? Here's a broker who specialises in this. Need a solicitor? Here's one we've worked with 20 times.

AI role: Intelligent matching - which investor's buy criteria match this deal? Which broker has the best rates for this property type? The matching gets smarter with every transaction.

4

Execute

Manage the process through to completion. Coordinate the solicitors, surveyors, brokers and contractors. Chase progress, flag delays and keep everyone on track. The client doesn't have to project-manage their own transaction - we handle that.

AI role: Automated progress tracking, document generation, timeline management, compliance checklists. Every transaction follows a structured workflow.

5

Support

The relationship doesn't end at completion. Ongoing portfolio monitoring, regulatory alerts, market updates and periodic reviews. A landlord who converted one property to SA might want to do the same with another. An investor who bought their first deal will want their second. This is where one-off transactions become long-term relationships.

AI role: Portfolio analytics, compliance monitoring, market intelligence alerts, maintenance prediction. Proactive, not reactive.

AI in Action

This isn't AI for the sake of it. These are specific, practical ways artificial intelligence makes the business faster, smarter and more scalable than a traditional operation could ever be.

The honest truth about AI in 2026: most businesses that claim to "use AI" are using it for basic tasks - chatbots, email drafting, document summaries. What we're proposing is different. That means it can do in minutes what would take a traditional consultancy days, and it can scale without needing to hire a large team.

Three AI-powered sourcing strategies

These are the specific ideas Rital identified - each one represents a genuine competitive edge because they use publicly available data in ways that most property businesses simply aren't doing yet.

1. Companies House - Distressed Business Intelligence

Companies House data is public and accessible via API. AI can systematically scan for companies with property-related activity that are showing signs of financial distress - administration, liquidation proceedings, winding-up petitions, overdue filings. Cross-reference these with Land Registry data to identify which distressed companies actually own property.

How it works in practice: The system runs daily scans, flagging newly distressed companies with property assets. Each flag comes with a data pack - company details, property titles, estimated values, nature of distress. Instead of manually trawling public records, we get a curated pipeline of potential BMV portfolio acquisitions delivered every morning.

2. Probate Property Identification

Probate records are publicly available through the Probate Registry. Land Registry titles are searchable. AI can cross-reference these datasets to identify properties where the owner has recently passed away - flagging potential probate properties before they ever reach the open market.

How it works in practice: The system identifies matches and generates an outreach package - a sensitively worded letter explaining what we do and how we can help the family. The tone is critical: this is about offering support during a difficult time, not about profit. Every communication is reviewed before sending. The approach only works if it's done with genuine care and respect.
Important note: This strategy requires careful legal review around data protection (GDPR) and the ethical handling of bereavement communications. We recommend taking proper legal advice before implementing this and establishing a clear ethical framework for all outreach. Done right, this is a valuable service to families. Done wrong, it damages the brand irreparably.

3. Investor and Private Finance Matching

Finding investors with capital to deploy and matching them with appropriate deals. AI can profile investor preferences (location, price range, strategy, risk appetite) and automatically match them to new opportunities as they come through the pipeline.

How it works in practice: An investor registers their criteria. When a deal comes in that matches, they get an alert with a full analysis pack - valuation, comparable sales, yield projections, refurb estimates, regulatory compliance status. The matching gets smarter over time as the system learns which deals each investor actually proceeds with.
Important note: Depending on how the investor matching and private finance raising is structured, this may require FCA authorisation. The plan is to build this properly from day one - working with FCA-approved individuals and ensuring full compliance. Over time, the business becomes a fully authorised service. This is a trust and compliance-first operation, always.

Key Questions for Friday

These are the things we need to discuss and start making decisions on. Some I have a view on, some I need your input. Click each question to see the context.

1. What's the business structure?

+

New limited company? Partnership? How does Rital's existing SA company fit in - separate entity that feeds into the network, or part of the same business? How does Adam's equity stake work - shareholder in the new company, or a different arrangement? These decisions affect tax, liability, future investment and how profits flow. Worth having an accountant's view before finalising.

2. Who does what?

+

The working assumption is Rital operates the business day-to-day and Adam provides strategy, AI capability and network connections. But the detail matters. Who handles client relationships? Who manages the professional network? Who does the deal analysis? Who makes pricing decisions? Getting clear on roles early prevents confusion later. This doesn't need to be rigid - it needs to be honest.

3. What's the compliance position on investor matching and private finance?

+

If the business is matching investors with deals or raising private finance, FCA authorisation may be required depending on how it's structured. There are exemptions (introducing to authorised persons, certain professional investor categories) but this needs proper legal advice before launching. The approach should be: start with activities that don't require FCA authorisation, bring in FCA-approved individuals as the business grows, and build towards full authorisation over time. Compliance first, always.

4. How do we build the network?

+

The network is the competitive advantage - but it starts empty. What contacts does Rital already have from her SA business? What does Adam bring from his property background? Who are the first solicitors, brokers and surveyors we approach? Is there a vetting process for network members? How do we incentivise them to work with us? The network strategy is probably the most important operational decision we make early on.

5. What's the brand and market positioning?

+

"Rital & Adam Dragons" is a working title. What's the actual brand? The name should communicate trust, professionalism and property expertise without being generic. The tone matters too - are we the friendly neighbourhood property advisor, or the slick data-driven operation? The answer probably depends on who we're talking to. Landlords need warmth and trust. Investors need credibility and deal quality. The brand has to work for both.

6. What launches first?

+

With 24 potential revenue streams and a big vision, the question is: what do we actually do in month one? My recommendation would be to start with the advisory (portfolio health checks and strategy consultancy) plus Rital's existing SA management as the first network node. These require the least setup and start generating revenue and case studies immediately. The AI sourcing tools and investor matching build in parallel. But this is a conversation - Rital may have a different view on where the quickest wins are.

7. What's the ethical framework for probate sourcing?

+

We both agree this is a real opportunity handled with real care. Before implementing the probate sourcing AI, we need to define the rules: minimum waiting period after death before contact? Review process for every letter before it's sent? Opt-out mechanism? Tone and language standards? Legal review on GDPR compliance? This should be documented and non-negotiable. It protects families and it protects the brand.

8. What does success look like in 12 months?

+

This is probably the most useful question for Friday. If we fast-forward a year - what does the business look like? How many clients? How many deals? What revenue? How big is the network? What's the team? Having a shared picture of where we're heading makes every decision along the way easier. It doesn't need to be a detailed financial plan - it needs to be an honest conversation about ambition and expectations.

9. What are the risks and how do we manage them?

+

Every business has risks. The main ones here: regulatory changes that shift the market (already happening - but could accelerate), FCA compliance if we get the structure wrong, reputational risk from the probate approach, dependency on the network (if a key partner leaves), market downturn reducing deal volume, and the perennial startup risk of trying to do too much too soon. None of these are reasons not to do it. All of them are reasons to plan properly.

10. How does Adam's investment work?

+

Adam is bringing AI capability, strategic consultancy, network connections and capital. What does that translate to in equity terms? What's the vesting arrangement? What are the obligations on both sides? What happens if one party wants out? These conversations are always better to have early, when the relationship is strong and the stakes are low. A simple shareholder agreement drafted by a solicitor protects everyone.

A note on how to use Friday: We don't need to answer all ten questions in one sitting. The most important ones to get aligned on are: what launches first (question 6), who does what (question 2) and what success looks like in 12 months (question 8). Everything else can follow once those foundations are set.
